Micro-fabrication
Photolithography is a microfabrication process allowing to build microstructures as small as several microns.

 This process gives a lot of freedom regarding the shape of the structures to build. Images shown here are used to check the quality of the manufacturing process and are realized using a SEM (Scanning electron microscope).
Super-hydrophobic and slippery surfaces
Surfaces wettability can be controlled by the addition of microstructures and/or by chemical treatments.

 Super-hydrophobic surfaces strongly repel water and avoid its contact as much as possible. The ability to make a surface super-hydrophobic has many applications, ranging from water protection to fog collection and oil/water separation.

Slippery surfaces are porous surfaces imbibed with small amounts of oil. Droplets of liquids deposited on them show a very high mobility, making those surfaces useful for accurate droplets control as well as for surface protection (rust, biofouling, icing, ...).
Wettability gradients
Surfaces with builtin wettability gradients can be used to control the motion of droplets deposited on their surfaces.

 The ability to control the motion of droplets on long distances, and on tilted surfaces opens new possibilities for microfluidics devices. It allows notably the fabrication of complex circuits for droplets (including merging, sorting and positioning droplets).

Non-chaotic attractor
Attractor representing the dynamics of an acoustic-driven cavity flow.

 This attractor is obtained using the delay embedding method on the velocity evolution at a specific point of the flow. This method allows to extract the underlying dynamics, despite the apparent complexity of the velocity time-series. This dynamics is represented here by a non-chaotic torus. Related article
Chaotic attractor
Attractor representing the dynamics of an acoustic-driven cavity flow.

 For high acoustic forcing, the flow adopt more complex behaviors, represented in the phase space by a chaotic attractor. This chaotic dynamics implies that the flow is, due to sensitivity to initial conditions, unpredictable on the long term. Related article
Poincare section
Poincare section of an attractor representing the dynamics of an acoustic-driven cavity flow.

 Right: representation of the attractor in the three-dimensional phase space. Left: Density map of the Poincare section in one of the main attractor direction. The Poincare section helps reducing the dimension number and allow to get a qualitative insight on attractor properties (fractality, dimensions, chaotic behavior). Related article

M.d.C Mignot
Pr. Rivière
Pr. Perkins
Velocity field in a HSV
2D Velocity field obtained by PIV upstream from an emerging obstacle.

 Measure is taken in the vertical plane of symmetry, upstream from the obstacle, and show two swirling structures: (i) the HSV, created in the bottom region by the boundary layer separation, and (ii) the superior TFC, at the corner between the obstacle and the free-surface, induced by the first one.
Vorticity fields in a HSV
Instantaneous vorticity field obtained by PIV upstream from an emerging obstacle.

 The vortices composing the horseshoe vortex are clearly visible as minima of vorticity. This representation allows to track the vortices position in time and to have quantitative information on the flow dynamics.
PIVF in supercritical flows
PIVF (Particle Image Velocimetry by Fluorescence) of a super-critical flow $Fr>1$ around an emerging obstacle.

 Measure is taken in the vertical plane of symmetry, upstream from the obstacle, to observe both the horseshoe vortex (bottom) and the hydraulic jump (top). PIVF allows to remove the scattered part of the laser emission and to make measurement in the presence of a strongly deformed and agitated free-surface.

Flow Dynamics

Oscillation direction and amplitude
Oscillation directions and amplitudes induced by the horseshoe vortex in a flow around an emerging obstacle.

 Measure is taken in the horizontal plane, near the bottom, to observe the evolution of the horseshoe vortex as it roll around the obstacle. Extracting the oscillation due to a structure (here the HSV) at a particular frequency allows to visualize the impact of this structure on the surrounding flow.

Critical points
Critical points and associated streamlines in a 2D velocity field.

 Critical points allow to study quantitatively the flow topology and dynamics by tracking their positions in time. Associated streamlines separate the flow in different regions and allow to identify the feeding and the fed regions.

Vortex density maps
Clockwise and counter-clockwise vortex presence density maps.

 A kernel density estimator (KDE) is used to access the vortices preferential positions and trajectories. Here the vortices appears at the beginning of the shear layer (starting at 0,0) and are then advected downstream, ending in the flow (bottom part) or in the cavity (upper part).

 The horseshoe vortex is here in a Complex regime, characterized by an irregular (but periodical) alternation of vortex behavior.
